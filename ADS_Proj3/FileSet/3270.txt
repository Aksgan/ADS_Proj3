This is a town in the municipality of [[Ferlach]] in [[Carinthia (state)|Carinthia]] of [[Austria]]. He moved to the United States in the 1950s and lived in the Cleveland area most of his life.<ref name="control"/><ref name="nytimes"/><ref name="intech"/> ==Programmable logic controller== {{Main| Programmable logic controller}} [[Image:PLC Control Panel.png|thumb|PLC Control Panel]] Struger is sometimes known as the "father of the programmable logic controller" as is [[Dick Morley]].<ref name="intech">{{cite web|url= http://www.isa.org/InTechTemplate.cfm?Section=article_index1&template=/ContentManagement/ContentDisplay.cfm&ContentID=28889|title= Leaders of the Pack|accessdate= 2008-06-20}}</ref><ref name="expert">{{cite web|url=http://www.encyclopedia.com/doc/1P2-4462023.html|title= Odo Struger, automation expert |accessdate= 2008-06-20}}</ref> Struger was involved in the invention of the [[Allen-Bradley]] [[programmable logic controller]] (PLC) during 1958 to 1960.<ref name="control"/><ref name="nytimes"/><ref name="anzovin">Anzovin, p. 100, item # 2189. ''Programmable logic controller was invented by the Austrian-born American engineer Odo J. Struger in 1958-60 at the Allen-Bradley company in Milwaukee, WI, USA. A programmable logic controller, or PLC, is a simple electronic device that allows precise numerical control of machinery. It is widely used to control everything from washing machines to roller coaster to automated manufacturing equipment.''</ref> Struger is often called the father of '''Allen-Bradley's''' programmable logic controller ("PLC") and credited with creating that acronym.<ref name="intech"/> The abbreviation "PLC" and the term "Programmable Logic Controller" are registered trademarks of the Allen-Bradley Company.<ref name="what">{{cite web|url= http://www.machine-information-systems.com/PLC.html|title= What IS a PLC?|accessdate= 2008-06-21}}</ref> Rockwell (Allen-Bradley) became the programmable logic controller device leader in the United States during the tenure of Struger.<ref name="short">{{cite web|url= http://www.jimpinto.com/writings/automationhistory.html|title= A short history of Automation growth |accessdate= 2008-06-20}}</ref> This is a device that is part of computers used today in nearly every machine automated factory worldwide.<ref name="expert"/> It is an electronic device that allows precise numerical control of machinery.<ref name="anzovin"/><ref name="what"/> It makes possible modern factory automation, as well as heating, ventilation, air-conditioning, plastic injection molding machines, commercial washing machines, and amusement park rides. It is even found in lavish stage effects of Broadway productions.<ref name="control"/><ref name="nytimes"/> The devices are designed to work in real time, taking in information, processing it and producing commands to control a mechanical process as it happens.<ref name="nytimes"/><ref name="what"/> The Programmable Logic Controller ("PLC") came about as a replacement for automatic control systems that used tens and hundreds (maybe even thousands) of relays, motor driven cam timers, and rotary sequencers.<ref name="what"/> Murray Slovick (Spectrum editor, a magazine of the Institute for Electrical and Electronics Engineers) has been reported as saying, {{cquote| ''Programmable logic controllers are what industry uses to automate factories. They are used to control a wide range of equipment, everything from heating, ventilation, air-conditioning, to plastic injection molding machines to commercial washing machines.''<ref name="nytimes"/>}} William Little (Rockwell Automation chief technical officer) quoted to reporters, {{cquote| ''Programmable logic controllers are pervasive. They show up in the road production of 'Phantom of the Opera' and in rides at Disney World.''<ref name="nytimes"/>}} Struger based his computer technology on a concept developed in his [[doctoral dissertation]] at the [[Vienna University of Technology]] titled, ''The Process for Quantitative Handling of Positioning Errors of Numerical Control Machines.''<ref name="nytimes"/> He obtained a doctorate degree in 1970 at the Vienna University of Technology from this thesis.<ref name="control"/> The device Struger invented is essentially a rugged industrial computer that controls machinery.<ref name="nytimes"/><ref name="what"/> It is in most machine factories throughout the world today, especially those of high technology. The programmable logic controller computer technology is now a billion-dollar-a-year industry.<ref name="nytimes"/> ==Career== Struger left the Vienna University of Technology in 1956 for a job at [[Asea Brown Boveri]], a Swiss power company.<ref name="nytimes"/> In 1958 he became a research engineer at [[Allen-Bradley]] in Milwaukee. Here he headed a team that developed the programmable logic controller.<ref name="nytimes"/> Struger later became a vice president for Allen-Bradley and held that position until he retired in 1998. He worked for the company for nearly 40 years.<ref name="control"/><ref name="nytimes"/><ref name="intech"/> Struger played a leadership role in developing the [[National Electrical Manufacturers Association]] (NEMA) standard for PLCs and its international successor, [[IEC 61131]].<ref name="intech"/> Struger patented means for detecting malfunction conditions at the inputs to a logic gate. One was for the "Fault Detecting and Fault Propagating Logic Gate." That patent was U.S. Pat. No. 3,743,855 issued July 3, 1973. Another control circuit employing fault mode logic gates was U.S. Pat. No. 3,751,684, issued to Struger on August 7, 1973.<ref name="patents1">{{cite web|url= http://www.freepatentsonline.com/3992636.html|title= Digital input circuit with fault detection means |accessdate= 2008-06-20}}</ref> Struger also received U.S. Pat. No. 3,240,951.<ref name="board">{{cite web|url= http://www.patentstorm.us/patents/4716497/description.html|title= Printed circuit board module patents |accessdate= 2008-06-20}}</ref><ref name="multiloop">{{cite web|url= http://www.freepatentsonline.com/3611101.html|title= MULTILOOP POSITIONING CONTROL SYSTEM|accessdate= 2008-06-20}}</ref> Struger also received Canadian patents.<ref name="canadian">{{cite web|url= http://patents.ic.gc.ca/cipo/cpd/en/patent/926472/summary.html|title= Canadian Patents Database|accessdate= 2008-06-20}}</ref> Struger has received and been involved with several other patents.<ref name="otherpat">{{cite web|url= http://www.patentlens.net/patentlens/search_ajax.cgi?patnum=US+4553224|title= Inventors|accessdate= 2008-06-20}}</ref> ==Retirement and death== Struger retired in 1997 as Allen-Bradley's vice president of technology.<ref name="control"/><ref name="intech"/> He died on December 8, 1998, in Cleveland, Ohio, at the age of 67 of [[thyroid cancer]]. His wife was Marlene. He had two sons, Andre, of Farmington Hills, Michigan., and Gregory, of Newbury, Ohio.
