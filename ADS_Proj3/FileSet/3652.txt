circuits== A coupled LC circuit, as shown in the Figure, consists of two LC circuits coupled by a mutual inductance between the inductors. This is equivalent to a pair of harmonic oscillators with a kinetic coupling term. The Lagrangian for coupled LC circuits is as follows: :<math> L= \frac{1}{2} L_1 \frac{dQ_1}{dt} ^2 + \frac{1}{2} L_2 \frac{dQ_2}{dt} ^2+ m \frac{dQ_1}{dt} \frac{dQ_2}{dt} - \frac{Q_1^2}{2C_1} - \frac{Q_2^2}{2C_2} </math> The Hamiltonian is found using a Legendre transform of the above Lagrangian. :<math> H=\frac{1}{2} L_1 \frac{dQ_1}{dt} ^2 + \frac{1}{2} L_2 \frac{dQ_2}{dt} ^2+ m \frac{dQ_1}{dt} \frac{dQ_2}{dt} + \frac{Q_1^2}{2C_1} + \frac{Q_2^2}{2C_2} </math> Promoting the observables to quantum mechanical operators yields the following Schr√∂dinger equation. :<math>E \psi = - \frac{\hbar^2}{2 L_1} \frac{d^2 \psi}{d Q_1^2} - \frac{\hbar^2}{2 L_2} \frac{d^2 \psi}{d Q_2^2}- \frac{\hbar^2}{m} \frac{d^2 \psi}{d Q_1 d Q_2} +\frac{1}{2} L_1 \omega ^2 Q_1^2 \psi+\frac{1}{2} L_2 \omega ^2 Q_2^2 \psi </math> One cannot proceed further using the above coordinates because of the coupled term. However, a coordinate transformation from the wave function as a function of both charges to the wave function as a function of the charge difference <math> Q_d </math>, where <math> Q_d =Q_1-Q_2</math> and a "Center-of-Mass" coordinate <math> Q_c </math>(for lack of a better term), the above Hamiltonian can be solved using the Separation of Variables technique. The CM coordinate is as seen below: :<math> Q_c=\frac{L_1 Q_1 +L_2 Q_2}{L_1 + L_2} </math> The Hamiltonian under the new coordinate system is as follows: :<math>E \psi = - \frac{\hbar^2 (1-\lambda)}{2 (L_1+L_2)} \frac{d^2 \psi}{d Q_c^2} - \frac{\hbar^2 (1-\lambda)}{2 \mu} \frac{d^2 \psi}{d Q_d^2}+\frac{1}{2} \mu \omega ^2 Q_d^2 \psi</math> In the above equation <math> \lambda </math> is equal to <math> \frac{2 m}{L_1 + L_2} </math> and <math> \mu </math> equals the reduced inductance. The separation of variables technique yields two equations, one for the "CM" coordinate that is the differential equation of a free particle, and the other for the charge difference coordinate, which is the Scrodinger equation for a harmonic oscillator. :<math>E \psi_c = - \frac{\hbar^2 (1-\lambda)}{2 (L_1+L_2)} \frac{d^2 \psi_c}{d Q_c^2} </math> :<math>E \psi_d = - \frac{\hbar^2 (1-\lambda)}{2 \mu} \frac{d^2 \psi_d}{d Q_d^2}+\frac{1}{2} \mu \omega ^2 Q_d^2 \psi_d </math> The solution for the first differential equation once the time dependence is appended resembles a plane wave, while the solution of the second differential equation is seen above. ==Hamiltonian mechanics== ===Classical case=== Stored energy (Hamiltonian) for classical LC circuit: :<math>\mathcal{H} = \frac{q^2(t)}{2C} + \frac{p^2(t)}{2L} \ </math> Hamiltonian's equations: :<math>\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}(q,p)}{\partial q} = \frac{q(t)}{C} = -\dot{p}(t) \ </math> :<math>\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}(q,p)}{\partial p} = \frac{p(t)}{L} = -\dot{q}(t) \ </math>, where <math>q(t) = Cv(t) \ </math> stored capacitor charge (or electric flux) and <math>p(t) = Li(t) \ </math> magnetic momentum (magnetic flux), <math>v(t) - \ </math> capacitor voltage and <math>i(t) - \ </math> inductance current, <math>t - \ </math> time variable. Nonzero initial conditions: At <math>q(0), p(0) \ </math> we shall have oscillation frequency: :<math>\omega = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} \ </math>, and wave impedance of the LC circuit (whithout dissipation): :<math>\rho = \sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} \ </math> Hamiltonian's equations solutions: At <math>t \ge 0 \ </math> we shall have the following values of charges, magnetic flux and energy: :<math>\mathbf{q} = q(0) + j\frac{p(0)}{\omega L} \ </math> :<math><q(t) = Re[\mathbf{q}e^{-j\omega t}] \ </math> :<math>p(t) = Im[\omega L\mathbf{q}e^{-j\omega t}] \ </math> :<math>\mathcal{H} = \frac{|\mathbf{q}|^2}{2C} = constant \ </math> ====Definition of the Phasor==== In the general case the wave amplitudes can be defined in the complex space :<math>a(t) = a_1(t) + ja_2(t) \ </math> where <math>j = \sqrt{-1} \ </math>. <math>a_1(t) = \frac{q(t)}{q(0)} \ </math>, where <math>q(0) = D(0)S_C = \sqrt{\frac{2\hbar}{\rho}} \ </math> - electric charge at zero time, <math>S_C - \ </math> capacitance area. <math>a_2(t) = \frac{p(t)}{p(0)} \ </math>, where <math>p(0) = \sqrt{2\hbar \rho} \ </math> - magnetic flux at zero time, <math>S_L - \ </math> inductance area. Note that, at the equal area elements :<math>S_C = S_L = S_q \ </math> we shall have the following relationship for the wave impedance: :<math>\rho = \sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} = \frac{q(0)}{p(0)}</math>. Wave amplitude and energy could be defined as: :<math>a(t) = ae^{-j\omega t} \ </math> :<math>\mathcal{H} = \hbar \omega [a_1^2(t) + a_2^2(t)] = \hbar \omega |a|^2 \ </math>. ===Quantum case=== In the quantum case we have the following definition for momentum operator: :<math>\hat{p} = -j\hbar \frac{\partial }{\partial q}</math> Momentum and charge operators produce the following commutator: :<math>[\hat{q},\hat{p}] = j\hbar \ </math>. Amplitude operator can be defined as: :<math>\hat {a} = \hat {a_1} + j\hat {a_2} = \frac{\hat {q}}{q_0} + j\frac{\hat {p}}{p_0} \ </math>, and phazor: :<math>\hat {a(t)} = \hat {a}e^{-j\omega t} \ </math>. Hamilton's operator will be: :<math>\hat{\mathcal{H}} \hbar \omega [\hat{a}_1^2(t) + \hat {a}_2^2(t)] = \hbar \omega [\hat {a}\hat {a}^\dagger + 1/2] \ </math> Amplitudes commutators: :<math>[\hat{a}_1(t),\hat{a}_2(t)] = j/2 \ </math> :<math>[\hat{a}(t),\hat{a}^\dagger(t)] = 1 \ </math>. Heisenberg uncertainty principle: :<math>< \Delta \hat{a}_1^2(t) > < \Delta \hat{a}_2^2(t) > \ge \frac{1}{16} \ </math>. ====Wave impedance of free space==== When wave impedance of quantum LC circuit takes the value of free spece :<math>\rho_0 = \sqrt{\frac{L_0}{C_0}} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_0}{\epsilon_0}} = 2\alpha \frac{h}{e^2}</math>, where <math>e - \ </math> electron charge and <math>\alpha - \ </math> fine structure constant, then "electric" and "magnetic" fluxes at zero time point will be: :<math>q_0 = \sqrt{\frac{2\hbar}{\rho_0}} = \frac{e}{\sqrt{2\pi \alpha}} \ </math> :<math>p_0 = \sqrt{2\hbar \rho_0} = \phi_0\sqrt{\frac{2\alpha}{\pi}} \ </math>, where <math>\phi_0 = \frac{h}{e} - \ </math> magnetic flux quantum. ==Quantum LC circuit paradox== ===General formulation=== In the classical case the energy of LC circuit will be: :<math>W_{LC} = W_C + W_L, \ </math> where <math>W_C = 0.5CV_C^2 - \ </math> capacitance energy, and <math>W_L = 0.5LI_L^2- \ </math> inductance energy.
