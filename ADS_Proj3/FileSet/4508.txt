Specifically, high frequency TENS activates delta-opioid receptors both in the spinal cord and supraspinally (in the medulla) while low frequency TENS activates beta-opioid receptors both in the spinal cord and supraspinally{{Citation needed|date=May 2010}}. Further high frequency TENS reduces excitation of central neurons{{Citation needed|date=May 2010}} that transmit nociceptive information, reduces release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate) and increases the release of inhibitory neurotransmitters (GABA) in the spinal cord, and activates muscarinic receptors centrally to produce analgesia (in effect, temporarily blocking the [[Gate control theory of pain|pain gate]]){{Citation needed|date=May 2010}}. Low frequency TENS also releases serotonin and activates serotonin receptors in the spinal cord, releases GABA, and activates muscarinic receptors to reduce excitability of nociceptive neurons in the spinal cord{{Citation needed|date=January 2010}}. In [[palliative care]] and [[pain medicine]], TENS units are used in an attempt to temporarily alleviate [[neuropathic pain]] <ref>{{Cite journal|author=Forst T, Nguyen M, Forst S, Disselhoff B, Pohlmann T, Pfützner A |title=Impact of low frequency transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation on symptomatic diabetic neuropathy using the new Salutaris device |journal=Diabetes Nutr. Some patients benefit from this approach, while others do not, depending on individual differences, <ref>{{Cite journal|author=Chesterton LS, Barlas P, Foster NE, Baxter GD, Wright CC |title=Gender differences in pressure pain threshold in healthy humans |journal=Pain |volume=101 |issue=3 |pages=259–66 |year=2003 |month=Feb |pmid=12583868 |url=http://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S0304395902003305 |doi=10.1016/S0304-3959(02)00330-5}}</ref> and pain threshold. <ref>{{Cite journal|author=Chesterton LS, Foster NE, Wright CC, Baxter GD, Barlas P |title=Effects of TENS frequency, intensity and stimulation site parameter manipulation on pressure pain thresholds in healthy human subjects |journal=Pain |volume=106 |issue=1-2 |pages=73–80 |year=2003 |month=Nov |pmid=14581113 |url=http://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S0304395903002926 |doi=10.1016/S0304-3959(03)00292-6}}</ref> Use of TENS causes cell death{{Citation needed|reason=statement lacks references; after perusing dozens of medical articles on TENS I could not find supporting research.|date=November 2010}} at the site of application and should not be used long term. The number of cells that die is insignificant and most patients heal without complaints. TENS is more effective at relieving chronic pain, where as when used on new injuries it sometimes aggravates the pain.
