NIST selected 51 entries for the Round 1.<ref name="Round1" /> 14 of them advanced to Round 2,<ref name="Round2" /> from which 5 finalists were selected. ===Finalists=== "NIST has selected five SHA-3 candidate algorithms to advance to the third (and final) round" <ref>http://csrc.nist.gov/groups/ST/hash/email_list.html</ref>: * [[BLAKE (hash function)|BLAKE]] * [[Grøstl]] ([[Lars Knudsen|Knudsen]] et al.) * [[JH (hash function)|JH]] * [[Keccak]] (Keccak team, [[Joan Daemen|Daemen]] et al.) * [[Skein (hash function)|Skein]] ([[Bruce Schneier|Schneier]] et al.) NIST noted some factors that figured into its selection as it announced the finalists:<ref>[http://webcache.googleusercontent.com/search?sourceid=chrome&ie=UTF-8&q=cache:crypto.junod.info/2010/12/10/sha-3-finalists-announced-by-nist/ SHA-3 Finalists Announced by NIST], blog post quoting NIST's announcement in full.</ref> * Performance: "A couple of algorithms were wounded or eliminated by very large [hardware gate] area requirements – it seemed that the area they required precluded their use in too much of the potential application space." * Security: "We preferred to be conservative about security, and in some cases did not select algorithms with exceptional performance, largely because something about them made us 'nervous,' even though we knew of no clear attack against the full algorithm." * Analysis: "NIST eliminated several algorithms because of the extent of their second-round tweaks or because of a relative lack of reported cryptanalysis – either tended to create the suspicion that the design might not yet be fully tested and mature." * Diversity: The finalists included hashes based on different constructions, including the HAIFA and sponge hash constructions, and hashes with different sources of nonlinearity, including S-boxes and the interaction between addition and XOR.
