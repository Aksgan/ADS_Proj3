An appeal will also succeed if the subscriber can show that the rightsholder or ISP broke Ofcom's regulatory code in any way.<ref name="dea_sec13" /> ===== Obligations on ISPs and copyright holders ===== The Act gives Ofcom the responsibility of enforcing ISPs' obligations. A fine of up to £250,000 can be levied on those in contravention; that limit can be raised by the Secretary of State with Parliament's consent.<ref name="dea_sec14">{{cite web|url=http://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/24/section/14?view=plain|title=Digital Economy Act, section 14: Enforcement of obligations|publisher=[[UK Government]]|accessdate=July 15, 2010}}</ref> The Act also allows the Secretary of State to define, with the consent of Parliament, the "sharing of costs" incurred from the Act's legal processes. The order can require reimbursements to be made by rightsholders and/or ISPs, and in the case of appeals, subscribers.<ref name="dea_sec15">{{cite web|url=http://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/24/section/15?view=plain|title=Digital Economy Act, section 15: Sharing of costs|publisher=[[UK Government]]|accessdate=July 15, 2010}}</ref> ==== Section 17 and 18 - Blocking Internet locations ==== The Act allows the Secretary of State - with the consent of the [[Lord Chancellor]], [[House of Lords|upper]] and [[House of Commons of the United Kingdom |lower]] houses of Parliament and a court of law - to block access to a location on the Internet "from which a substantial amount of material has been, is being or is likely to be made available in infringement of copyright", or a location which "facilitates" such behaviour.<ref name="dea_sec17">{{cite web|url=http://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/24/section/17?view=plain|title=Digital Economy Act, section 17: Power to make provision about injunctions preventing access to locations on the internet|publisher=[[UK Government]]|accessdate=July 2, 2010}}</ref> In deciding whether to grant an injunction, the court is required to consider: * Steps taken by the operator of the location to prevent infringement * Steps taken by the copyright owner to facilitate lawful access to the material * Any representations made by a Minister of the Crown * Whether the injunction would be likely to have a disproportionate effect on any person’s legitimate interests * The importance of freedom of expression The Secretary of State must be satisfied before making the request that the location is "having a serious adverse effect on businesses or consumers", that the injunction "is a proportionate way to address that effect", and that "making the regulations would not prejudice national security or the prevention or detection of crime".{{Citation needed|date=September 2010}} === Other provisions === Other provisions in the Act include an amendment to the [[Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988]] to increase the criminal liability for "making or dealing with infringing articles" and "making, dealing with or using illicit recordings" to a maximum of £50,000, so long as it is done during the course of a business.{{Citation needed|date=September 2010}} Regulation of content is also in the Act, which adopts the [[Pan European Game Information]] (PEGI) standard for video game ratings. The UK government is also able directly to intervene to control the use of the UK's domain name space, currently overseen by the independent body [[Nominet]].{{Citation needed|date=September 2010}} The Act also includes changes to [[Digital radio in the United Kingdom|DAB radio in the United Kingdom]] including the reorganisation and merging of some of the local [[DAB ensemble]]s<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.billboard.biz/bbbiz/content_display/industry/e3i0040e099982664b6b69422dd757955dd|title=Race Against Time For U.K. Digital Bill|date=2010-02-10|last=Paine|first=Andre|accessdate=2010-03-14|publisher=Billboard}}</ref> and a requirement for a digital switchover of terrestrial radio in the United Kingdom.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.telegraph.co.uk/technology/6616161/Digital-Economy-Bill-No-date-for-radio-digital-switchover.html|title=Digital Economy Bill: No date for radio digital switchover|accessdate=2010-03-14|date=2009-11-20|last=Barnett|first=Emma|publisher=The Telegraph | location=London}}</ref> Further measures included: * The management of <code>[[.uk]]</code> Internet domain registries * The functions of the [[Channel4#Channel Four Television Corporation|Channel Four Television Corporation]] * The regulation of television and radio services * The regulation of the use of the electromagnetic spectrum * The [[Video Recordings Act 1984]] * Public lending right in relation to electronic publications (Authors receive a 10p royalty for physical books lent from UK libraries but not ebooks. It was announced in the 2009 [[speech from the throne|Queen's Speech]] before the United Kingdom parliament.<ref>[http://www.publications.parliament.uk/pa/ld200910/ldhansrd/text/91118-0001.htm#09111813000012 "Ensure the communications infrastructure is fit for the digital age"] in the Queen's Speech</ref> The first reading of the bill was presented to the [[House of Commons of the United Kingdom]] on 16 March 2010.<ref>http://services.parliament.uk/bills/2009-10/digitaleconomy.html</ref> The bill received its second reading on the 6 April 2010 in a Commons debate and was given support by the [[Conservative Party (UK)|Conservative]] front bench<ref>{{cite news| url=http://www.guardian.co.uk/technology/2010/apr/06/digital-economy-bill-conservative-support | location=London | work=The Guardian | title=Digital economy bill gets reluctant Conservative support | first=Mark | last=Sweney | date=2010-04-06}}</ref> and as expected<ref>{{cite news| url=http://www.guardian.co.uk/technology/2010/mar/25/digital-economy-bill-commons | location=London | work=The Guardian | title=Digital economy bill to be pushed through parliament next month | first=Charles | last=Arthur | date=2010-03-25}}</ref> was not debated at length in the Commons, and was instead pushed through in the "[[Dissolution of the Parliament of the United Kingdom|wash up]]". It passed [[third reading]] on 7 April<ref>{{cite news| url=http://www.guardian.co.uk/technology/blog/2010/apr/07/digital-economy-bill-internet | location=London | work=The Guardian | title=Digital Economy bill: liveblogging the crucial third reading | first=Charles | last=Arthur | date=2010-04-07}}</ref> with [[Royal Assent]] granted on 8 April.
